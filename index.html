About

This page brings together six county-level indicators: foreign direct investment, employment & unemployment rates, average salaries, GDP per capita, and high school graduation rates. Rather than reading through tables of statistics, users can immediately see which counties are thriving, which are struggling, and how different measures relate to each other across Hungary's twenty counties.

Use the Map / X Variable dropdown in the header to change what the choropleth map and chart's horizontal axis display. Adding a Y Variable switches the visualization on the right from a ranked bar chart to a scatter plot, revealing relationships between two indicators at once. Selecting a Size Variable (only available once a Y variable is chosen) upgrades the scatter plot to a bubble chart, encoding a third dimension through Flannery-scaled circle area. Click any county on the map or any element in the chart to pin it, and its full attribute profile appears in the header for easy reference. Click a second county to compare them side by side. Click a selected county again to deselect it. Hovering over any element highlights its counterpart across both views simultaneously.

In nearly every variable is the outsized dominance of Budapest. Its FDI dwarfs every other county by an order of magnitude, and its GDP per capita and salary figures sit far above the national distribution. On the second tier, the northwestern counties of Győr-Moson-Sopron and Komárom-Esztergom consistently rank high in employment, salary, and investment, reflecting their proximity to the Austrian border and strong manufacturing base. The eastern and southeastern counties, particularly Szabolcs-Szatmár-Bereg and Nógrád, show consistently lower scores across economic indicators alongside higher unemployment, creating a clear west-east prosperity gradient. Interestingly, high school graduation rates do not mirror economic output cleanly: southern counties like Csongrád-Csanád and Zala post relatively high graduation rates despite modest GDP figures, suggesting that educational attainment alone does not predict economic development at the county level.
